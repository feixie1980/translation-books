Chapter 2 — Mai Minghe Rescued from Hallucination?

"You live alone,"

the social worker unfolded a small brochure and showed it to her, saying: "If you fall, no one will know — that's too dangerous. You'd be better off wearing one around your neck; it won't get in the way, don't you think?"

That was many years ago now.

Mai Minghe still remembered the advertisement: the product's name was blunt and to the point — "Life Alert."

In the image, a white-haired woman lay on the floor, with two lines of small text beneath her: "Help me! I've fallen and I can't get up!"

Her older cousin, in her eighties, had shattered her pelvis in a fall and never left her bed again; once a person is bedridden, their days are numbered.

What year had she passed? Mai Minghe couldn't remember anymore.

The legs that had once carried her leaping onto rooftops, running and roughhousing — in the blink of an eye, she now had to be wary of them betraying her even when she simply got out of bed.

The body that had followed her all her life was gradually becoming something foreign; she was like a stiff wheel turning slower with each revolution, powerless to change course.

"The Emergency Response System operates twenty-four hours a day, three hundred and sixty-five days a year," the social worker explained, worried she couldn't read the small print on the brochure. "No matter the emergency, press it and someone will come — let me see — oh, they promise a response within thirty minutes at most, and there are participating hospitals nearby."

Mai Minghe took the brochure and studied the pictures for a moment.

She had scrimped and saved every penny her whole life, yet now money had suddenly lost its weight, prices nothing more than curved little symbols.

"I'll take this one then," she said, pointing to the third model. "This one is the nicest-looking. Like a necklace pendant."

The social worker burst out laughing. "All right — it'll definitely look good on you."

Though it looked nothing like one, she wore it as a necklace all the same; she had worn it that way for years, and it wasn't until the thief entered the living room to make his phone call that she pressed the Life Alert for the very first time.

The thief probably hadn't expected that the "necklace" on her chest was an alarm — the young and the old live in two nearly sealed-off worlds; without any real need to deal with elderly people, very few know what their lives look like, let alone the things they see and use every day.

From the moment she had the thief looking for a poem… she had stalled for everything she was worth, and already bought herself more than ten minutes.

Still no one had come, yet she was on the verge of giving out.

For some reason, the cold tube defied gravity — standing perfectly upright on her chest without anyone holding it, humming steadily away. The sound of the poem grew more and more distant, and she sank into wave after wave of dizzy darkness, deeper and deeper.

From the depths of that darkness, hallucinations floated up like fragments of a shattered dream.

Strange — they say that when a person is near death, they see the memories of a lifetime; but Mai Minghe saw only another version of herself, a tube connected to her chest just the same, walking step by step toward the television, then suddenly lunging forward and crashing into the screen.

She was startled by her own actions, and faintly heard a dull, murky thud from inside her chest.

With her eyes half-open and half-shut, hallucination and reality overlapped; the self in the hallucination crashed into the old television in the corner of the room again and again, the screen blooming with spider-web cracks each time.

The hallucination was finally shattered by a sharp shout — a strange, deep, gravelly voice called out: "What are you doing? Who are you?"

Mai Minghe jolted, and slowly opened her eyes.

Through blurred vision, she saw a jet-black, gleaming tube standing upright on her chest, as though it were more alive than she was, pressing her heavily down into the bed.

The next moment, the tube vanished.

The thief grabbed the tube behind his back; startled but quick-witted, he turned on the two who had just burst into the bedroom: "Who are you? Why have you suddenly broken into my grandmother's house?"

Two people strode in through the doorway, switched on the lights, and drove out the night; the room flooded white-bright, revealing a bedroom full of decay, the smell of medicine, cracks in the walls, and the steady whoosh of the oxygen machine.

They were both in blue-and-white uniforms — nurse aides.

"Your grandmother?"

The male nurse aide looked at Mai Minghe, then cast a suspicious glance at the thief. "Her? She's your grandmother?"

"I'm two generations mixed — it doesn't show much anymore." The thief seemed to notice the difference in their complexions and ethnicities; he was quick on his feet and said immediately: "Look, I have dark hair. Who are you two?"

"We're emergency nurse aides. We came because we received an alert signal — we thought she had an emergency." The other, a woman's voice, explained. "According to our records, she lives alone."

The thief had completely composed himself by now.

"Yes," he said, gently patting Mai Minghe's arm as if to comfort her, and told the nurse aides: "You responded very promptly, you got here fast — thank you. But there's been a small misunderstanding. I'm in Blackmoor City on business and staying with my grandmother temporarily. She's getting on in years; her mind isn't always clear. She must have forgotten I was coming and thought I was a stranger, so she triggered the alert. She's fine."

The female nurse aide walked to the bedside, lifted Mai Minghe's eyelids, checked her over, and asked softly: "Ma'am, are you all right?"

Mai Minghe opened her mouth, wanting to say something, but only the faintest breath passed between her lips. The female nurse aide had seen it often enough and wasn't surprised that she couldn't speak; she began listening to her heartbeat.

"Would you mind showing me some identification?" the male nurse aide asked the thief, still slightly uncertain.

"Of course," the thief said, and right in front of the nurse aides, brazenly kicked the black device with its tube under Mai Minghe's bed, muttering as he did: "Why is the vacuum cleaner left in this spot…" Then: "Hang on, let me go to the next room to get my wallet. My driver's license is in there."

The bed she now lay in was a hospital care bed with four wheels; the space underneath was completely open — just right for storing things.

"Oh — the phone," the thief had only taken two steps when he turned back, flashed a smile at the female nurse aide still examining Mai Minghe, and said: "I almost forgot. My grandmother sometimes gets frightened for no reason; you have to play her some poetry or music to settle her down."

The female nurse aide's gaze traveled past Mai Minghe to the phone screen and lingered there for a moment; her expression softened noticeably.

Mai Minghe thought: a thief who breaks in and plays poetry for the homeowner — who would believe that? It looked far more like something a good grandchild would do.

Since she wasn't dead yet, the thief must not have succeeded in taking whatever it was he wanted from inside her — right?

Right, otherwise he wouldn't still be here playing the devoted grandson.

But if the nurse aides had arrived a few minutes later, he would have succeeded.

Nobody knew that death had just been sitting beside her, leaning over to gaze down at her; she had never been so close to death before, and it was the first time she had ever had a dying hallucination…

Wait — the television!

Even Mai Minghe herself hadn't expected it, but the moment she thought of the hallucination just now, she was suddenly seized by an urge from nowhere — she wanted to roll out of bed and go touch the television.

She had to get over there, had to get there quickly and look…

Driven by one surging wave of urgency after another, Mai Minghe was so agitated she nearly cried out.

The dark television screen had become the only thing in the world with any weight; it pressed heavily into her field of vision and drew her toward it, while the room, the nurse aides, and the thief were all as thin as paper, as though they might float away at any moment.

Mai Minghe strained to lift her hand toward the television.

"Ma'am, what is it? What do you need?" The female nurse aide followed the direction of her raised hand and glanced back. "Do you want to watch television?"

The door to the storage room next door opened and then closed, as though the thief had truly gone in and retrieved something. Just as the female nurse aide was asking her question, he happened to return and handed his identification to the male nurse aide; the latter looked it over, and when he spoke again, his tone and posture had both relaxed.

Mai Minghe let out a sound of negation through her nose, her hand still pointing at the television.

"I'll turn it on for you," the female nurse aide said — well-meaning if puzzled, and without waiting for Mai Minghe's answer, she switched on the television.

A daytime news commentary program was being rebroadcast; the host was going over and over the sudden death of Blackmoor City magnate Wellesley from the day before, recounting his business triumphs and philanthropic works.

"No… no." Mai Minghe finally managed to produce sound again — a quiet relief, she noted; good, she could speak, which meant her strength was returning.

The thief shot her a quick glance.

He turned back to the male nurse aide almost immediately and picked up where he had left off: "…My grandmother is getting on in years, and the more confused she gets, the more stubborn she becomes. There's no family in Blackmoor City who can look after her…"

Such a young person, and the lies just kept coming one after another.

Telling the nurse aides that this man was not her grandson would probably do no good. An ordinary thief would never stay at the scene to negotiate; from the nurse aides' perspective, he looked far less like a thief than she looked like a senile old woman.

Never mind — right now, the television mattered more, was more urgent than he was.

"Help me… help me over there," Mai Minghe pointed at the television and said quietly to the female nurse aide: "Please."

The thief glanced at her again, then looked at the television.

Before he could open his mouth, the male nurse aide's question pulled his attention away: "When we came in, you were bent over holding a tube — what were you doing?"

"You don't have to go over there — you can watch television from here," the female nurse aide said, looking a little awkward.

The thief was in the middle of explaining: "Grandmother woke up calling out in the middle of the night; I got up and came to check on her, and found the vacuum cleaner hose had fallen onto the bed."

"Please," Mai Minghe gripped the female nurse aide's hand and fixed her brown eyes on hers, pleading: "Help me over there, please. I… I want to sit over there for a bit."

She was playing the part of a confused old woman more convincingly than ever.

"All right, I'll help you." The female nurse aide gave in. "Can you sit up?"

"Yes, yes."

The thief glanced at Mai Minghe once more, stepped forward with an air of concern, and took her other arm — conveniently removing himself from the male nurse aide's questioning as well. "Grandma, what do you want to go over there for? Just a quick look, and then you have to come back and sleep, all right?"

Mai Minghe ignored him. He seemed like a perfectly decent young man — so what on earth was he doing with himself?

With the two of them supporting her on either side, she shuffled over to the television; her own pale, ghostly half-reflection floated on the screen, then disappeared as the broadcast cut to footage of the Wellesley Family Estate.

She understood now.

So there really had been a reason for the hallucination, Mai Minghe thought.

No wonder she had felt so urgently drawn to come over here — well, she should have realized sooner; at her age, the mind just wasn't what it used to be.

"I'll go get a chair," the female nurse aide said, and let go of Mai Minghe.

That left only the thief holding her. He kept a loose, light grip on Mai Minghe's frail arm, as though afraid to use any real force.

"If everything is fine, then we'll be heading off." The male nurse aide stood in the doorway and asked the thief: "Can you help her back to bed yourself in a bit?"

The thief let out a quiet breath of relief. He nodded repeatedly and said: "Of course — sorry to have made you come all this way…"

While he was speaking, Mai Minghe suddenly pulled her arm free, let herself fall backward, and toppled toward the television screen.

She should have thought of it sooner — her hallucination had been showing her how to save herself.

If she fell against the television right in front of the nurse aides, they would have no choice but to take her to the hospital, no matter what the thief claimed to be — isn't that right? At the hospital, with plenty of people around, she would be safe.

From the corner of her eye, she saw the thief's head snap toward her, but he was a step too slow; it was too late. Mai Minghe heard the dull, heavy crack of her skull striking the television, and then the room filled with startled cries and the sound of rushing footsteps.

The female social worker seemed to call out something from very far away, but Mai Minghe couldn't make it out —

"Where did she go?"
