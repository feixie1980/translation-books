Chapter 1 — Mai Minghe: I Want to Pick More Daisies

When Mai Minghe woke, the room was dark, and through the gap below the curtains there was still only night.

She slept lightly, waking several times each night, but tonight the reason was different. A small, hard sound had roused her from the steady "whoosh—whoosh" of background noise that filled the room.

What was making that sound?

She was still thick with drowsiness, her mind slow to catch up.

The metallic scraping and clicking continued, and then came a sharp snap—the lock cylinder jumped, and the door was pushed open with a long, creaking groan. The hinges hadn't been oiled in years; they were stiff and sluggish as an old man's joints, and they carved a raw gash through the silent night.

Ah. Someone had broken in.

If her mother ever found out, there was no telling what kind of scolding she'd get—a girl living alone, and she couldn't even think to wedge a chair against the door at night?

Her mother had always been uneasy about the world: she checked the gas four or five times before leaving the house, always looked in the back seat before getting into a car, and never once allowed her to nod or smile back at strangers.

Mai Minghe had always thought her mother was a little too anxious. She hadn't expected that someone would actually break into her home tonight.

The sounds that followed were hard to make out, but dimly, a figure walked in. Through the crack at the bottom of the door, a beam of light swept back and forth—a flashlight.

Unfamiliar footsteps moved with a muffled tread through the living room, occasionally producing a creak. Mai Minghe recognized that sound: it was the floorboard at the kitchen doorway. One year a water pipe had burst, and the boards there had warped from the soaking; ever since, they had frequently voiced their sharp complaints.

Having a burglar break in was quite an unusual thing, really.

She closed her eyes again and listened intently to the burglar's movements.

He opened the door to the adjacent room first—a very small, narrow space that had never been put to much use. It had once been painted as a nursery, then repurposed as a study, then converted into a cloakroom, yet not one of those functions had ever truly been realized; now it was simply a storage room thick with dust.

One glance was enough to see that everything piled inside was junk, not worth rummaging through. Sure enough, the burglar's footsteps retreated at once, and the door shut with a dull thud.

In truth, if you searched the entire building, you probably wouldn't find a single thing of any value.

Things did go missing often enough in this low-rent apartment block. Once, Mai Minghe had failed to latch her door properly, and by the time she noticed, a pair of ankle boots from the rack by the entrance had vanished. It had made her furious enough to knock on every neighbor's door and ask around, but nothing came of it. Still, as much as things disappeared, it was rare for an outside burglar to go to the trouble of picking a lock and forcing his way in to steal from people who had nothing to steal.

It was funny—a trivial, inconsequential thing, yet she remembered it so clearly. Those boots had been calf leather, brown and smooth and gleaming, with a smart look about them. She had never managed to find another pair she liked as much.

Whatever he wanted to take, let him take it. She didn't have anything worth making a fuss about.

The burglar moved to the bedroom doorway. The door swung open, and the flashlight beam swept in, finding Mai Minghe immediately.

The harsh white light shone bright through her eyelids and lit up her face, yet she remained perfectly still, eyes closed, feigning sleep.

An unfamiliar voice let out an involuntary "Huh?"—as though surprised.

Mai Minghe held herself still and said nothing.

The burglar's footsteps padded into the room. He made a circuit of the bedroom without even opening the bedside-table drawer, then left again.

Searching so carelessly—how could anyone steal anything that way? Mai Minghe found herself anxious on his behalf. A person had to have a little patience in whatever they did; they had to be willing to take the trouble.

A minute or two passed. He came back in and crossed to her bedside, and the warm, living heat of a person hovered over the bed.

"Hey," the man's voice called, and a hand gave Mai Minghe's bony shoulder a nudge. "Hey, wake up."

It seemed there was no point in pretending to sleep any longer.

What did he want with her awake? If he was looking for something valuable, couldn't he just look for it himself? Not only a thief, but a lazy one at that—people these days, really.

Mai Minghe opened her eyes. A dark figure was bent over her, looming above the bed.

The flashlight hung low along the bedside, and the burglar's face was swallowed by shadow. At the edge of the light, she could just make out a pale throat, its Adam's apple rising and falling as he spoke.

"Don't be frightened. I just want to ask you something. Is there someone called Mai Minghe living here?"

"…That's me. Do we know each other?"

The man drew a breath and straightened up, taking in the shape beneath the covers.

"You—you're the one?" He seemed unwilling to believe it, and raised the flashlight, shining it over Mai Minghe's face several times without any attempt at consideration.

She hadn't looked in a mirror in a long time and had no idea what she looked like now—it couldn't be good, at any rate.

"What is it?" she asked quietly.

The man stood with his mouth half open, as though he was still digesting something.

She could see that he had been hoping she wasn't Mai Minghe; for he immediately asked again, as if she might not have heard: "Mai—the mai from 'wild wheat.' Mai—Ming—He."

"That's me," Mai Minghe said, with a faint smile. "There's no one else."

The man stared at her.

After a few seconds, he asked, "Old lady, how old are you?"

How old? Even Mai Minghe had to think about it.

She couldn't say exactly when it had started, but she'd been losing track of her age for years now, sometimes even mistakenly thinking of herself as a young girl.

A year, one year at a time—it didn't sound like much, yet it was countless tiny moments, like snowflakes falling without end, accumulating into a lifetime before you noticed.

Reach out and grab a handful of snow, and you don't know when it fell. A stretch of life floats up, and she can no longer remember how long ago it belongs to.

The social worker had said that at her age—eighty-six—Mai Minghe's mind was still relatively clear, unlike some elderly people…

"Eighty-six?" Mai Minghe turned to the social worker in her memory. "You must be mistaken. I'm nowhere near that old."

The room fell quiet again, and the "whoosh—whoosh" of background noise rose once more.

The burglar seemed to notice it only now. He pointed to the small plastic tube inserted in Mai Minghe's nose and asked, "Is that… are you on oxygen?"

"Hmm?" She sometimes forgot it was there. "Oh, yes. Terribly annoying."

The burglar let out a sigh. He set the flashlight down on the edge of the bed; its halo fell on the opposite wall, cutting the room into light and shadow—a bright ring of warmth on one side, and on the other, dim decline.

"Silly of me—I forgot to ask. Do you drink tea?" said Mai Minghe. It had been so long since anyone had come by that she'd nearly forgotten to be a host. "Getting up is a bit of an effort for me… there's tea and biscuits in the kitchen—help yourself."

The burglar grew visibly uncomfortable, as though this was not a scene he had prepared for.

"No, thank you," he said, hesitating for a moment before adding, "But… would you like some? I can bring you a cup."

Mai Minghe declined and thanked him, and for a brief moment the homeowner and the burglar exchanged polite courtesies.

The burglar clasped his hands together and stared at the television in the corner of the room for a long moment, then asked it: "Your health isn't good?"

"It's quite all right—I can still get out of bed and walk on my own."

"…What about meals? Does a son or daughter bring them?"

"I don't have any children," she said, with a small smile. "I don't eat much anyway—it doesn't take much fuss… A social worker brings me vegetables and eggs now and then."

"A social worker? Do you have no relatives in Blackmoor City?"

"I'm used to it," Mai Minghe said. "I never had many relatives or friends to begin with, and they all died before me. Dear, do you watch television?"

The burglar seemed to find the question absurd. He was silent for a moment, then suddenly pulled out his phone, glanced at it, and walked quickly out of the room.

"Hello?"

So he'd gone to take a call.

"Yes, I've found her… No, I haven't done it yet." Here the burglar hesitated. "Did you know she's an old woman? Eighty-six. It wasn't in the file."

The response from the other end was brief, because the burglar spoke again almost immediately.

"Yes," the burglar said, making no effort to lower his voice—perhaps he didn't see the need. "But… does it have to be hers? She's not the only one with a passage. Besides, we don't even know what her passage is like… Taking it from someone her age—even a young person would have a hard time surviving that. What if she can't hold on and dies?"

The person on the phone was displeased; even Mai Minghe seemed to catch the faint rise of his voice from across the room.

The burglar took it in silence for a while, then at last, like a student confessing a mistake, he said, "No… I understand. I'm sorry. I'll get it."

Mai Minghe was sometimes startled by her own hands.

Thick, folded skin, layered over every bone, with veins and blood vessels raised up beneath it, almost cold to the touch—they belonged to someone other than the hands she remembered as her own.

With this hand that seemed not to be hers, she groped beneath the covers, found the pendant resting on her chest, and held it tight.

This time, the burglar spoke as soon as he came through the door: "I'm sorry, old lady. There's something I need to take from you."

Mai Minghe could just barely make out his face—a bright, clean-looking young man, somewhere in his twenties or thirties, still practically a boy; you'd never guess he was a bad person without being told.

"What do you want?" Mai Minghe asked. "Honestly, I have no use for anything anymore—take whatever you like. There's a silver brooch in the drawer…"

"No, I don't want valuables," the burglar interrupted her. "I need to take something from inside your body."

"An organ?" Mai Minghe was curious. Her organs, she would have thought, held no value worth taking.

"No, no, don't ask. You wouldn't understand even if I explained."

"Then—will I die?" Mai Minghe asked. "You said so just now."

The burglar was caught off guard by the question. He was silent for two seconds, then said, "I don't know… possibly."

Mai Minghe lay quietly for a moment, looking at the crack in the ceiling.

"If you must take it, then take it," she said softly. "Only… before you do, could you grant me one wish?"

Somehow the burglar had already come to stand beside her.

"What wish?"

"I used to work at a library. The pay was very little, but I liked libraries—I was happy there… Once, when my shift ended, the librarian told me there was an old book they were retiring: it hadn't sold at the clearance price, so she gave it to me to take home."

The burglar rested his hand on her pillow.

Near his feet was a dark, hulking shape—some kind of machine—that he must have brought in at some point. Mai Minghe was only noticing it now.

"When I looked, it turned out to be a collection of poetry. I flipped through it idly when I got home," Mai Minghe murmured. "I read it, put it down, and eventually I don't even know where the book ended up. But somehow… these past few years, I keep thinking about one of the poems inside."

"Which poem?"

"You young people—don't you all have those… clever phones?"

"Smartphones," the burglar corrected.

If she had had a grandchild, they might have corrected her the same way.

"I've heard they have everything in them?"

"…More or less."

"Could you find that poem for me?" Mai Minghe pleaded. "I'd like to hear it one more time."

The burglar looked down at her and suddenly seemed a little at a loss. He pulled a tissue from the box on the bedside table and pressed it into her hand. As Mai Minghe stared at it blankly, not understanding, he said, "All right, don't cry—I'll find it for you. Do you remember the title?"

She was crying?

So even at this age, she could not accept death.

Or perhaps it was that even at this age, she had not yet truly lived.

"It's called… something with 'daisy' in it." Mai Minghe deliberately left out half the title.

A smartphone had plenty of things in it, but finding one particular poem wasn't easy—especially since "daisy" was such a common image—and it took quite some time.

After finding several poems that were all wrong, the burglar finally lost patience: "Old lady, you don't remember the name—that's not my fault. I still have to get back and report in, so you—"

"I remember now," Mai Minghe said quickly.

This young man who had come to take something from her was, in all likelihood, the last person in her life who could let her hear a poem one more time.

Even if she could delay no longer and had to face death, at least she could listen to a little more poetry—that wasn't so bad.

"'I'd Pick More Daisies'… I think that's the name of the poem."

The young man glanced at her, said nothing, and tapped at the screen a few times.

This time, he quickly found the poem that Mai Minghe had kept returning to in her mind.

"I found a reading video for you," he said, setting the phone on the edge of the bed.

In the quiet room thick with the smell of medicine and decay, the ad before the video burst into cheerful noise, announcing a special deal at Little Caesars Pizza.

"The poem comes on after the ad—just listen. You listen, I'll get to work."

He bent down and pulled a tube from the dark, hulking machine.

…If I had my life to live over,

I would try to make more mistakes.

I would relax,

I would be more supple,

I would be sillier than I have been this trip.

Mai Minghe felt the side of her face grow damp.

She had spent her whole life in anxious calculation, just like her parents. During wartime inflation, she had carefully rationed every meal; in peacetime, she kept the family accounts every week. When she was a child, her family ran a small shop, and no matter how much she craved it, she never dared to steal so much as a single piece of candy from the shelf.

As she grew older she came upon many crossroads overgrown with wildflowers, but she never once set foot off the path.

People were strange—everyone knew they only had this one life, yet each day was lived as though there was still endless time, and one could always come back and try again.

The thief lifted the blanket and pressed a cool tube against Mai Minghe's chest, and only then did she dimly realize the thing looked something like an old-fashioned vacuum cleaner.

"What's this?" The thief picked up the pendant on her chest, glanced at it, and without waiting for Mai Minghe to answer, moved it aside away from the tube's mouth. Young people generally didn't recognize it—they had no idea what the thing was for.

Luckily, he didn't know.

Amid the poem, he pressed the tube's mouth against Mai Minghe's skin-and-bones chest. He started the machine, and a low hum unlike anything she had ever heard began to spiral slowly through the room.

…If I could do it again, I would travel lighter than I have.

If I could do it again, I would start barefoot earlier in the spring and stay that way later in the fall.

I would go to more dances,

I would ride more merry-go-rounds,

I would pick more daisies.

I would not take so many things so seriously.

In the final moment as the darkness of death closed over Mai Minghe, she heard, faintly, the front door of the home being slammed open, and urgent footsteps and voices came rushing in all at once.

Carried for years in the womb, and finally delivered—an ugly child at that…

"I'd Pick More Daisies" is a real poem. The poet wrote it when she was 85—one year younger than my daughter. The inspiration didn't actually come from the poem; I had already started writing before I suddenly remembered it, and it felt like a perfect fit.

Oh my, it's been so long since I updated and rambled like this—I feel almost embarrassed; I don't quite know what to say.

Thank you for coming back.
